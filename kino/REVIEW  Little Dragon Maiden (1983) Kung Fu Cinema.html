REVIEW: Little Dragon Maiden (1983)

By Mark Pollard

As a young vagabond is introduced to the martial world and begins learning kung fu from various masters, he is mistreated as a result of his father’s crimes, finds love with the mysterious Dragon Maiden, and ultimately rises to face a group of menacing Mongols.

Old school meets new wave as kung fu movie legends Chen Kuan-tai, Lo Lieh, and Guk Fung dominate a supporting cast led by successful Cantopop singer, heartthrob, and award-winning actor Leslie Chung. Known for his many romantic leading roles, he also cemented a loyal fan base for popular roles in later New Wave action classics like A Better Tomorrow, A Chinese Ghost Story, and Ashes of Time. Little Dragon Maiden marks his first martial arts film, which itself followed a trend towards merging fantasy elements from silent era martial arts films with a fresh-faced image. The film combines the wire-fu, eccentric characters, and costumed creatures from Buddha’s Palm with the theme of rebellious young lovers breaking ranks with tradition as seen in Journey of the Doomed.

The story moves along at a quick pace as Yang Guo (Leslie Chung) appears just in time to witness a female fighter dispense with a number of challengers. A rarely seen perspective of the martial world is shown as he becomes one of several scavengers fighting over the belongings of the dead fighters once the woman leaves. She shows up again later as Li Mochou (Tim Lei), martial sister to the Dragon Maiden. Animal lovers be warned, a cherished puppy she possesses shows up again as well, skinned and chopped up for Lo Lieh’s stew! While the skin is a fake, a centipede sliced open and a bird chopped in half are not. Lo Lieh plays a crazy master of Toad kung fu who “runs” around on his hands and suffers from short-term memory loss. He takes Yang Guo on as his adopted son and begins to teach him his kung fu before running off. Guo must be the luckiest guy in the world for he just keeps running into helpful people and creatures throughout the film. Chen Kuan-tai plays his uncle who identifies him, takes him in, and puts him under the tutelage of a Wu Tang swordsman belonging to the Quanzhen Sect. But the swordsman has a grudge against Guo on account of his father who was considered a traitor to the clan. Repeatedly abused, Guo finally thumbs his nose at his master and seeks refuge on forbidden land where a beautiful Dragon Maiden (Yung Jing-jing) lives in seclusion with an elderly woman. Guo and the Maiden are destined to fall in love as they train in an esoteric and powerful kung fu style together minus their clothes (i.e. Cheung in his briefs and Yung covered by a scarf)! As the pair publicly snubs the traditional rules for unmarried couples to behave in the martial world, they are separated and Guo accidentally runs off a cliff in his love-torn grief. Oopsy! But like most fantasy swordplay films that can mean only one thing, our hero is about to discover some long-lost and helpful secret that is waiting just for him. In this case, it’s a giant condor with a pot belly who gives Guo a stout sword. Guo never shows the Maiden the same affection he dishes out for his new feathered friend. The condor’s friendship comes in handy when Guo returns in time to face the Mongols just as they threaten to kill the Maiden and his uncle.

Little Dragon Maiden is something of a patchwork film bringing together various elements that are interesting in of themselves, but make for an uneven and confusing experience. The story is never anchored by any central conflict and the fight with Mongols feels more like a sideshow than the main attraction that it should be. Leading the invaders initially is Goo Goon-chung, a veteran SB villain who isn’t a very imposing villain. Yet, he does play center stage in one of the better martial arts battles while wielding an iron fan to good effect. Showing an unfortunate influence from the independent kung fu movies of the day, Lung Tien-hsiang is brought in as the Mongol chief at the last moment to play the final villain. With no time or interest invested in the character, his only asset is a wicked, rotating saw blade that he wields with deadly precision. One luckless fighter gets cut in half from top to bottom while leaping in mid-air.

A series of lesser conflicts that devolve into soap opera territory make up most of the running time. The film’s central struggle is between Guo and two Wu Tang swordsmen, one of whom has a grudge harbored against his father and the other is enamored with the Dragon Maiden. There is also the Dragon Maiden’s martial sister who is jealous of her relationship with Guo, the lovers’ struggle against social restrictions, and Chen Kuan-tai’s struggle to steer Guo right. Some sickly sweet Cantopop planted in the soundtrack adds to this rambling and romantic story, thus creating a rather bland and dated viewing experience.

The liveliest elements in the film include Lo Lieh’s uncharacteristic crazy toad kung fu routine. He is even joined by Guk Fung in an equally uncharacteristic kung fu drunkard role. The birdman is by far the greatest oddity in the film. While I’m all for quirky characters in kung fu movies, they all seem like leftovers from better films. The wire-fu is all fantasy and does feature a few wild moments akin to the action found in Kung Fu Cult Master. But there is as much flying around as there is actual combat, and not a lot of that. It’s all done with technical proficiency, but nothing stands out.

In the end, nothing sinks a good fantasy swordplay film like a sappy romance. Unfortunately, Little Dragon Maiden also flounders too much with rehashed fantasy conventions to even be considered a good example of the genre. On the reverse, there is a decent acting performance from Leslie Cheung, as well as engaging cinematography and direction that maintains momentum from SB veteran Wa Saan. Yet this film is lightweight fluff that shows where the studio was headed during their transition to TV production, particularly where their top martial arts filmmakers Chang Cheh, Chor Yuen, and Lau Kar-leung were not involved.
