                              richard 23 days ago

                              I recently read Paul Davies' book "The Eerie Slience: Renewing our Search for Alien Intelligence". He refers to work done by Geoffrey Landis and then extended by Robin Hanson on modelling interstellar migration and colonization by a civilization based on "reasonable assumptions". It turns out this can be done using something called "percolation theory" and the results are (perhaps) surprising. The net result is not that every part of the galazy become colonised, but that there may be significant bypassed areas. The area of space colonized would tend to form a fractal pattern with gaps existing at all spatial scales. So, a possibility is that we find ourselves to be in a gap, at the moment. Note that this could explain the lack of visitation, but not necessarily the failure (to date) of SETI.

                                                                                                  Lyall Taylor 2 months ago

                                                                                                  What about the simple explanation that long distance space travel is simply not technologically feasible. It doesn't matter how technologically advanced a civilisation is if the task at hand is flat impossible. It can't be done no matter how many billions of years have elapsed since the dawn of life.

                                                                                                  Space is a vast and barren place, and energy (and other resources) are highly disbursed rather than concentrated. This is a potential problem, in an analogous way to how the second law of thermodynamics inevitably spells long term doom for life in the universe. Energy and other resources in the universe simply may not be abundant enough in sufficiently concentrated doses to support long distance space travel; keep any on-board inhabitants alive; and to support self-replicating space vessels. You have to generate a constant energy surplus throughout that postulated 3.7m years. Perhaps generating such an energy surplus in a cold, dark, and barren universe isn't possible?

                                                                                                  For me, this is the simplest and most obvious explanation - certainly relative to the more 'creative' explanations that are sometimes raised - but it is an explanation that is almost never proffered. I don't understand why. Occam's razor...
