Why nationalisation has fallen out of favour in Britain

Dec 2nd 2015, 23:44 by C.R. | LONDON

  • Add this article to your reading list by clicking this button

  • Tweet

AFTER almost half a century in the shadows, nationalisation appears to be making something of a comeback in Britain. Within weeks of his election as the leader of the Labour party, Jeremy Corbyn revealed his first official policy: to renationalise the rail network. But while this particular policy is popular with the public, over the past four decades policymakers have favoured privatisation over nationalisation. In the 1970s, industries that together produced over 10% of the country's GDPfrom public utilities to milk distributorswere in public hands. But under the governments of Edward Heath and Margaret Thatcher, Britain led the way in returning state-owned enterprises to the private sector. In the 1990s and 2000s, the Labour party followed Thatcher's lead, selling off stakes in Britain's air-traffic control system and nuclear-energy industry, even attempting to do the same with the Royal Mint. On November 25th, George Osborne, Britain's chancellor of the exchequer, said he would expand a programme of state-asset sales that is even bigger than that of the 1980s. Why does nationalisation remain out of favour?

In the 19th century, supporters of nationalisation argued that it boosted economic efficiency. State ownership benefited consumers, the argument went, by ensuring that companies had sufficient investment and profits were passed onto consumers through lower prices. Having a big single company rather than lots of little privately-owned ones would increase productivity through harnessing economies of scale. Even The Economist advocated in the 19th century that Britain's postal system and telegraph network should be in public hands. As a result, the state started to creep into more sectors of the economy. In the late-19th century, British utilities were bought up by local authorities. In the interwar period, the Conservative Party joined in, nationalising the BBC, the electricity-distribution network and the London Underground. And after the second world war, Labour governments went on a shopping spree, nationalising everything from coal mines to railways and travel agents.

There were significant efficiency improvements in nationalising the postal system and the telegraph network, but the nationalisations of the 20th century were much less successful. This was in part due to the rise of trade unions and the move towards a fully democratic political system. While nationalised companies were left to be minded by technocratic-minded officials in the 19th century, politicians with their eyes on elections started fiddling with them in the 20th. Whenever politicians needed tax cuts to win elections they tended to hack back investment in state-owned firms. They also had a free hand to bloat their payrolls in order to help governments achieve full employment in the economy overall, protected by a system of tariffs and monopolies designed to shield them from competition. And trade unions started to demand excessive pay rises and oppose efficiency improvements, knowing that the state, as owner, would always pay the bill to avoid a fuss at election time.

The most pernicious effect of this was to slow down the overall growth of the economy. In the heyday of nationalisation, between the 1940s and the 1970s, the British economy grew more slowly than its major competitors. As recent research has shown, since the 1980s the British economy has grown faster than its rivals, mainly due to supply-side reforms such as privatisation. By exposing firms to competition, state-owned firms became more efficient and were forced embrace innovation. Privatising others, meanwhile, is a way of making inefficient companies smaller, or non-existent (state-owned companies tend to stick around longer than they should). Privatisation, therefore, helped give Britain the edge over its competitors. Ordinary people have benefited from the process too. Domestic fuel prices have fallen by 30% in real terms since privatisation in the 1980s, for instance, benefiting the poor, who spend the greatest share of their income on it, more than the rich. There is still a need for more competition and reform in some industriesespecially Britain's rail networkbut the economic history of the last century suggests that any return to cosseted nationalised industries would be a mistake.

J the C Dec 3rd 2015 14:08 GMT

There are some industries which would probably be 'better', on balance, to nationalise: and often those are the industries that require investments at scale in long-term infrastructure. The private sector is traditionally quite bad at making these kinds of decision - discount rates mean that investments paying off more than 5 years away always seem poor compared to alternatives.

Beyond this, some essentials of modern life (widespread public transport, supplies of water, gas and power) are too fundamental to risk - which creates an implicit subsidy. Why invest in, say, a new power plant when the government can't afford to let the lights go out and will keep offering sweeteners until you build it? All told the costs of this can outweigh the efficiencies gained by having a smooth private-sector operator running essential services. A related but separate issue is that markets are efficient at maximising profits but this doesn't always equate to the desirable outcome: Healthcare is an archetypal example because it's hard to get a profit from preventative care, but very easy to get a profit from emergency care - though the former results in longer-lived, healthier workers contributing to the economy, and the latter results in a lot of needlessly crippled or dead patients. Even in more benign sectors like public transport, routes that aren't obviously profitable don't get operated, even if the lack of them means that some towns and villages are almost cut off from the rest of the country.

Finally, governments wisely have policy goals that are not simply profit-driven: decarbonisation of energy, for instance. Lacking direct control over large and dominant private companies means interventions become messy and far from optimal: the UK's electricity costs may have fallen significantly since the 1980s, but the energy infrastructure in Britain is in a critical and shoddy condition that we now have to invite the Chinese and French governments to fix for us. The costs of rebuilding the network are likely to push electricity prices up sharply in the next decade and beyond; it's cheap to run down existing infrastructure until it's unprofitable, but it's not beneficial in the long term.

Murchu_an_tEacnamai Dec 3rd 2015 12:28 GMT

If the privatisation of the electricity supply and gas industries in the '80s was such a success why did Ofgem consider it was necessary to refer both industries to the Competition and Markets Authority in June 2014? The layers of dysfunctional policy and regulation which have been added since have facilitated widespread gouging of citizens and small businesses as consumers and taxpayers. The Group of the "great and the good" selected by the CMA to investigate the supply businesses felt compelled to "do something" to reduce the gouging of small businesses and of the 70% or so of households on Standard Variable Tariffs (SVTs). They proposed a "regulated safeguard tariff" (RST) for these household consumers. Not surprisingly, the supply companies vociferously opposed the RST and some instead proposed that the evergreen nature of SVTs be phased out and replaced with time-limited contracts. The CMA, again not surprisingly, caved in to this pressure from the suppliers. So small businesses and those households which, for a variety of reasons, see no benefit in switching suppliers will continue to be gouged.

Advancing nationalisation as a viable alternative to privatisation is a typical facile debating technique deployed by The Economist. Apart perhaps from some geographically defined train franchises it would make absolutely no sense to contemplate nationalisation. However, what could be done, for example, in electricity and gas supply is to establish a statutory collective buyer of electricity and gas for those households who do not wish to participate in the faux retail market with the existing suppliers bidding to supply this collective buyer. Households who wished to participate in the dysfunctional retail market would be perfectly free to do so, but those who didn't would be supplied by the statutory collective buyer. The government has already designated National Grid as the effective single buyer of electricity generation capacity; a collective single buyer for households would be a much smaller step.

This would be a very effective way of putting some manners on the consumer-gouging energy suppliers. But I somehow doubt it would be welcomed by The Economist's subscriber base and all of the vested interests.
