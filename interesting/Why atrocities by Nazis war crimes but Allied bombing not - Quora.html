        World War II.
         
        It is almost impossible for a person born into the post World War II era, who has known only limited wars with limited objectives, to understand a world war fought against opponents who were at best amoral, and at worst the epitome of evil. You know about the Holocaust, the Warsaw Ghetto, and the Battle of Stalingrad, on the Nazi side. Are you aware of the Japanese enslavement of Korean women to serve as prostitutes for their soldiers, the Nanking Massacre, the treatment of civilians in countries occupied by Japan, the Bataan Death March, and the treatment of Allied POWs? You are, of course, aware of the German bombing of London, the blitzkrieg bombing of cities in Poland and Eastern Europe.[1]

        Are you aware that the atomic bombing of Hiroshima and Nagasaki did less damage and caused fewer deaths than the fire bombing of Tokyo? Are you aware that the civilian deaths from both the fire bombing and the atomic bombs were far less than those predicted if the Allies had to invade Japan? And that 1 million Allied casualties were predicted if the Allies had to invade? Not to mention millions of Japanese military casualties.

        Finally, are you aware that it was not clear, throughout much of World War II, that the Allies would win? And that if we lost, those atrocities listed in the first paragraph would be visited on us?

        What changed with World War II, was that the war was between nations and not just between the armies of nations. Civilians building tanks and planes, making bombs and bullets, and growing food for the Wehrmacht were helping the war effort just as much as, possibly more than, any single combatant. And that was true also in the U.S. where defense plants worked around the clock building everything that would be needed, including the ships to carry it in, to fight two different wars at the same time. As a 6-10-year-old, I helped collect old newspapers and scrap metal that were used in the war effort. I bought war stamps, 10 cents each, and when you got 187 of them you could trade them in for a War Bond that would be worth $25 in ten years.

        We did not start the war. In America, from the surprise attack on Pearl Harbor, December 7, 1941 and Hitler's declaration of war against the US a day later, the goal was to win the war as quickly as possible and get our men and women in uniform home. And that's what we did.

        “A sham employment of the legal process for the satisfaction of a thirst for revenge.”
        -Justice Radhabinod Pal, representing India in the International Military Tribunal for the Far East


        Justice Pal acknowledged that Japan committed war crimes (he described what the Japanese did in Nanjing as "devilish and fiendish"), but believed the scope of the trial should be expanded to include all countries, victors or losers, to really bring justice for all. He disagreed with the classification of "Class A War Crime" which had been created by the Allies just for this trial, and saw it as a cheap way for the victors to dissociate their own colonial crimes from those of Germany and Japan. In his eyes, all colonial invaders, even if they speak English, were equally deserving to be put on trial for their crimes.

        Justice Pal came from a unique perspective among his peers, he was from a country that had been invaded and colonized by one of the victors of World War 2. In his lifetime Justice Pal had seen unarmed independence protesters (including women and children) fired into with machine guns in the Jallianwala Bagh massacre.
        The man who ordered this killing was declared "Savior of India" by imperialist papers.

        Graeme Shimmin, amateur military historian.
         
        Telford Taylor, one of the American prosecutors at Nuremberg (and the chief prosecutor for the trials that followed the international trial) has said that he has never been able to find an adequate definition of "war crime" that included all of the things the Nazis were convicted of doing but does not include Dresden and Nagasaki.

        (He's not equating Dresden and Nagasaki to the Holocaust, of course. There are different levels of war crimes.)

        His argument, which I agree with, is that the Dresden raids were designed specifically to kill as many civilians as possible with no particular military gain, while, even if you believe that Hiroshima brought forward the end of the war by preventing a costly invasion of Japan, there was no excuse for dropping a second atomic bomb on Nagasaki just three days later.

        The problem? As Taylor said, the Allies were there to judge the Axis crimes, but "we would have needed a judge to descend from heaven to hold the winners to account."

        (EDIT: For anyone interested in World War II war crimes, I strongly recommend Taylor's book "The Anatomy of the Nuremberg Trials". It's not exactly a light read -  over 700 pages - but it goes into great detail about everything from the original discussions about holding a war crimes tribunal through the drafting of the Charter and the Indictment and the trial itself. It's fascinating, because it not only has the first-hand information of someone who was a major part of the process, but the perspective of five decades of reflection - Taylor didn't write the book until the 1990s.)

        This general issue was recognized by Judge Robert H. Jackson, Chief US prosecutor in the Nuremberg trials. He wrote a letter to President Truman in October 1945 saying that the Allies

        "have done or are doing some of the very things we are prosecuting the Germans for. The French are so violating the Geneva Convention in the treatment of prisoners of war that our command is taking back prisoners sent to them. We are prosecuting plunder and our Allies are practicing it. We say aggressive war is a crime and one of our allies asserts sovereignty over the Baltic States based on no title except conquest."

        He worried that this might hurt the international recognition of the trials. Because of this, he created a framework that would prohibit the Germans from discussing the causes of war, from pleading tu quoque ("you did it too") or from asserting that the law under which they were being tried was ex post facto, meaning that the law had not been established when the crimes were committed.

        John Carter, Misanthrope, Crank, Martyr
         
        What constitutes war crimes is generally determined by international treaties or by an international legislative body such as the UN or the League of Nations. When individuals are tried for war crimes, it is natural to ask what legitimacy the court has. What gives this court the legal right to try these individuals for these crimes in this manner?

        The legitimacy of the Nuremberg court was called into question from the outset and not just by the defendants. So, it is debatable if they had any right whatsoever to try the defendants on the basis of any principle other than "victors justice".

        But, assuming they did have such a right, the question asks if there were allied actions that would have been considered war crimes had they been conducted by the defeated Axis powers instead.

        The answer is yes. In fact, the Soviet Union was granted special exemption from prosecution for their role in carving up Poland and sharing it with Germany in the 1939 German-Soviet Non-aggression Pact. The reason the were granted this exception was so they could charge the Germans for their role in that same action.

        German Admiral Donitz was convicted for conducting unrestricted U-Boat warfare against the allies in clear violation of the 1936 London Naval Treaty. However, he was not punished for the crime as the US had done the same thing.

        However, most of the primary defendants of the Nuremberg trials were not members of the military and were not convicted based upon military actions such as civilian bombing. They were instead, members of the political leadership and they were primarily convicted for crimes like waging an aggressive war or crimes against humanity such as those crimes of the holocaust.

        So, it seems clear that an effort was made to convict the defendants for crimes that were not also committed by the allies. Crimes involving waging an aggressive war or conspiracy to wage an aggressive war were a good choice for prosecution for obvious reasons. Crimes that both sides engaged in were usually ignored or otherwise handled to avoid the appearance of the tribunal being a court of victors justice only.

        It is certainly true that there were crimes committed by the Allies that went unpunished just as their were crimes committed by the Axis powers that went unpunished.

        The answer is that it was not because the Allies won the war. This is made up by the "history is written by the victors" crowd because they claimed that the Allies did the same level of war crimes as the Nazis did due to area bombing of cities. When WWII started in 1939, there was no international treaty or instrument that prohibited the aerial bombardment on cities full of civilians. The 1907 Hague Convention was the only standard international law during WWII that set rules in place regarding the attack of civilian populations. However, the convention contained no specific air warfare provisions, it only prohibited the bombardment of undefended cities by naval artillery and field artillery as well as the unnecessary attack on civilians by ground forces since they have the ability to kill and shoot them at the close distance with careful aim and accuracy. The Nazis broke all of them; they rounded up civilians from other countries in Europe for no reason and shot them without any rational whatever ever, mostly in retaliation for guerrilla warfare, revenge, fun, or any reasons not justified by military and civilian necessity.

        For the Rape of Nanking, there is a very good reason why it was a war crime because the 1907 Hague Convention prohibited the unnecessary attack on civilians by ground forces. An excerpt from the 1907 Hague Convention IV  – The Laws and Customs of War on Land, SECTION II, MEANS OF INJURING THE ENEMY, SIEGES, AND BOMBARDMENTS:

        Article 23 - In addition to the prohibitions provided by special Conventions, it is especially forbidden.....(c) To kill or wound an enemy who, having laid down his arms, or having no longer means of defence, has surrendered at discretion.....(d) To declare that no quarter will be given.....(g) To destroy or seize the enemy's property, unless such destruction or seizure be imperatively demanded by the necessities of war.

        1907 Hague Convention IV – The Laws and Customs of War on Land, Section III, MILITARY AUTHORITY OVER THE TERRITORY OF THE HOSTILE STATE:

        Article 42 - Territory is considered occupied when it is actually placed under the authority of the hostile army. The occupation extends only to the territory where such authority has been established and can be exercised.

        Article 43 -  The authority of the legitimate power having in fact passed into the hands of the occupant, the latter shall take all the measures in his power to restore, and ensure, as far as possible, public order and safety, while respecting, unless absolutely prevented, the laws in force in the country.

        Article 46 - Family honour and rights, the lives of persons, and private property, as well as religious convictions and practice, must be respected.
        Private property cannot be confiscated.

        Article 47 - Pillage is formally forbidden.

        Article 50 - No general penalty, pecuniary or otherwise, shall be inflicted upon the population on account of the acts of individuals for which they cannot be regarded as jointly and severally responsible.

        Article 55 - The occupying State shall be regarded only as administrator and usufructuary of public buildings, real estate, forests, and agricultural estates belonging to the hostile State, and situated in the occupied country. It must safeguard the capital of these properties, and administer them in accordance with the rules of usufruct.

        Article 56 - The property of municipalities, that of institutions dedicated to religion, charity and education, the arts and sciences, even when State property, shall be treated as private property. All seizure of, destruction or wilful damage done to institutions of this character, historic monuments, works of art and science, is forbidden, and should be made the subject of legal proceedings.

        Japan was the signatory of the 1907 Hague Convention and flagrantly violated all of these provisions by inflicting massive killing of civilians and destruction of civilian targets in Nanking, making this atrocity a war crime. The destruction of enemy's property and the killing of civilians was extremely unnecessary to military gains and adds more resentment and suffering to basic humanity. The Japanese authorities had it's military occupying Nanking when the massacre happened because they either did nothing and/or issued orders that all Chinese civilians and POWs in the city were to be killed like animals, making the authorities equally responsible as their rapist soldiers. All of the Japanese atrocities and war crimes was sanctioned and endorsed by Tojo and members of the Japanese Royal Family in WWII.

        The Nazis did similar practices to civilians in other countries in Europe and this was also endorsed and sanctioned by Hitler's regime. The Holocaust was a war crime because it didn't serve any military purpose; to kill them off based on their race. It wasn't just German citizens of Jewish ancestry, many Jews and other ethic minorities in European countries conquered by the Nazis were taken from the homes and then shipped out to concentration camps to be slaughtered in the millions, therefore the rules laid down by the 1907 Hague Convention applied to the Holocaust.

        Aerial warfare wasn't subject to international law regulations in WWII because there was no law against aerial bombing attacks on civilians. After WW1 and in response to the bombing of Allied cities by German Zeppelins, a series of meetings were held at The Hague in 1922–23. A draft convention, promoted by the U.S., was proposed called The Hague Rules of Air Warfare, December 1922 – February 1923. There are number of articles which would have directly affected how nations used aerial bombardment and defended against it; these are articles 18, 22, and 24. It was, however, never adopted in legally binding form which means that no regulations on aerial warfare was established during WWII. http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deb...

        That's a major reason why the Luftwaffe and the Japanese Air Force wasn't prosecuted for their bombings of Allied cities at the IMT after WWII.         Joe Willmore

        Military History and Wars.
         
        I'm going to try and focus on the specifics of the question:  why Nazi war crimes were considered war crimes yet Allied area bombing and the use of the atom bomb weren't considered war crimes.

        First, the Germans engaged in bombing of cities (Rotterdam, London, Coventry City) yet to the best of my knowledge no Germans were tried or convicted for these acts as war criminals.  Any German bomber crew that was captured after bombing civilian targets wasn't tried for a war crime or put in a prison but instead was treated as a POW.  So it appears that the Allies did not define bombing of cities (either by Axis powers or by Allied powers) as a war crime.  You might have a case if Germans had been executed but Allied airmen had received medals--but that wasn't the case.  True--the deaths in the Luftwaffe bombings weren't as great as those with the RAF or USAAF but that's a matter of degree--the Allies were using more bombers with greater tonnage.

        Second, the atrocities committed by the Nazis were targeted and systematic.  They set up camps for jews and gypsies and homosexuals and sought to exterminate them from the earth (or at least their region of the earth).  There was no similar intent on the part of the Allies--they didn't intend to exterminate anyone, there was no systematic effort at killing.  The Nazis engaged in medical experiments on living, unwilling subjects.  The Allies engaged in no such activity.  There is pretty much consensus that the acts by the Nazis did indeed constitute atrocities.  And remember, if you want to argue that the Allied bombing of cities was an atrocity, keep in mind that neither German or Allied sources ever sought to try German Luftwaffe crews for bombing European cities.

        Third, why weren't the atom bombs considered war crimes?  I've actually already answered this question in detail in another thread but I'll give you a condensed answer here:
        1.  More Japanese died in conventional bombing than in the atomic bombs....it would be hard to classify conventional bombing as not a war crime but atomic bombs as one.
        2.  The Japanese were given an opportunity to surrender.  The Allies broadcast over open airwaves, they made a public declaration after Potsdam,  they reached out after each use of the atom bomb and they forwarded a surrender request through the Swiss Ambassador--none were acknowledged or responded to.  Instead, even after the Emperor announced that Japan would surrender (after the 2nd atomic bomb), the military attempted coup against the Emperor so that they could continue the war.  In fact, the US dropped leaflets on Japanese cities (including Hiroshima and Nagasaki before the atomic bombs) telling citizens to evacuate.
        3.  The Japanese had already ordered that all POWs were to be killed before they could be returned or recaptured.  Over 200,000 allied POWs were kept by the Japanese during the war.  Mortality rates were high but as the order to kill POWs came out, they begin doing things like herding them all into an air-raid shelter in the Philippines, blocking the doors, soaking it in gasoline and then setting it afire (they also did the same to a camp in Japan).  Even AFTER they surrendered, they beheaded 15 captured airmen at an airbase in Japan (Fukuoka).  My point is not that that Japanese were doing bad things so doing bad things in return was acceptable.  My point is that allowing the war to drag on would have likely resulted in the deaths of all or nearly all POWs by execution.  You think I'm being ridiculous?  The Japanese captured over 2 million Chinese soldiers and guerrillas during the fighting....56 remained alive at the end of the war.  56.  It's a very realistic position that unless the war ended suddenly (which required the atomic bomb or accelerated bombing of Japanese cities), the remaining POWs would be beheaded or burned alive in their cages.
        4.  Japan was prepared to continue fighting until the bitter end.  Forget the Kamikaze...those were "tame tactics" by what was to come.  They were teaching children to hold mines and rush at tanks.  They were giving women bamboo spears and training them to charge machine guns.  Russia invaded Sakhlin Island and annexed it at the end of the war before the Japanese surrendered.  If there had been no atom bomb, we would have seen an invasion of Japan by US troops in the south with Russia invading from the north (and in all likelihood Russia keeping Hokkaido, not returning it to Japan).  The US Dept. of War estimated US casualties for this invasion to be 1.4-4 million.  B/c of the nature of the fighting (house-to-house fighting for every inch of the country), Japan would have been completely leveled as a country.  Estimated Japanese casualties were 10 million.   And up to this point, Japan had engaged in a number of completely useless military battles (Tarawa, Iwo Jima, even Okinawa) where before combat even started, it was clear to the Japanese commander they couldn't win the battle, that they would lose, and yet fought almost to the death intentionally...b/c their strategy was to make engagements so bloody, to prove to the Allies that it would be too costly to actually invade Japan in hopes that this would lead the Allies from asking the Japanese to surrender (instead both sides would just agree to a cease fire).

        My point is not that the Japanese did bad things so they deserved what they got.  My point is that the Japanese fought the war in such a way that as bad as the atomic bombs were, they were preferable to allowing the war to drag on another 2-5 months or actually being forced to engage in an invasion.  If that had been the case, Japan would not be recognizable as a country today.

        As for the point about how you don't understand how Japan doesn't have vengeful feeling for American...compare what Germany went through after the war (the apologies, the reparations, the occupation) vs. what happened to Japan.  Japan has never really fully apologized for their role in WW-II.  Go read some South Korea or Chinese sources and you'll see there is still a lot of bitterness there...from war crimes committed by the Japanese to the "comfort women" to simply the lack of an apology (most official Japanese statements take about regret for the unpleasantness of the war as if this was a mutual affair and everyone is to blame).  To focus just on the atom bombs without understanding what lead to the decision to drop them (and the alternative to not dropping the bombs) is myopic.

        The problem with 'Morality' is that it is a luxury few can afford

        If a burglar entered your house, threatened your family, and stole your possessions you have four possible ways to react
        1. Do nothing and let the burglar get away with it
        2. Argue with the burglar and tell him it is wrong
        3. Try to stop him and get him out of the house
        4. Catch him and punish him so severely that he wished he had not tried to rob you
        Actions 1 and 2 will have no effect. Action 3 may or may not be successful, and even if it is, the burglar might try again, or move onto robbing someone else. Action 4 will give you some satisfaction, and may stop the burglar from his behaviour. If he can be disabled, then it might prevent him ever threatening anyone again

        The above choices apply to wars as well.

        The reasons given for the atomic bombing of Japan were that their soldiers and civilians would rather die than surrender. To this extent even if the military was defeated, the civilian population would carry on the fight.
